# Kanjiravanam Chronicles — Complete Storyline

> *A printable narrative document for story verification.*  
> *This is the game's story as it should feel — not gameplay beats, not mechanics. Just the story.*

---

## The World

On the southwestern edge of Kerala, where the backwater rivers thin into forest streams, there is a village called **Muruthikudi** — "Sacred Grove Hamlet." It is the kind of place where everybody knows everybody, where the chai stall has been open since before anyone can remember, where the houseboat captain has the same route he's had for thirty years.

At the edge of the village, the forest begins. Not a forest you walk into casually. **Kanjiravanam** — the sacred grove — is the kind of forest that has a reputation. Children don't play near it after dark. Old people don't talk about it much. The village has an instinct, passed down through generations, that the trees there are not just trees.

They are right.

---

## The Ancient Curse

Long ago — before memory, before written history — a portal between the mortal world and a forgotten demon realm was torn open somewhere deep inside Kanjiravanam. Ancient rituals, mishandled. The details are lost.

The gods sealed the portal the only way they could: by binding it to **five sacred trees** spread through the forest. Each tree became a lock. Each lock was given a guardian — a *kaavadukaaran*, a grove priest — whose family would tend the tree, perform the offerings, keep the ritual chain unbroken. As long as the five trees were tended, the seal held. The demons, known as the **Kazhukans**, were trapped on the other side.

For centuries, it held. The Kazhukans pressed against the seal from the other side, but the five trees stood, and the five families prayed, and the forest breathed clean.

---

## The King Below

Before Muruthikudi was a village, before Kanjiravanam had a name, before the Kazhukans were sealed away, Kerala had a king.

His name was **Mahabali** — Maveli, to his people. He was a demon by birth and a king by every measure that mattered: just, generous, tireless. Under his rule, there was no hunger, no fraud, no fear. Every person in his kingdom could look every other person in the eye. The rivers ran clean. The forests were cared for. People left their doors unlocked not because they were naive but because nothing needed to be stolen.

It was, by every account, the golden age.

Then a dwarf arrived at Maveli's court.

The dwarf was Vamana — a form taken by Vishnu, who had decided that a demon king this beloved was a threat to the cosmic order. Maveli welcomed him with the hospitality his kingdom was known for. The dwarf asked for three paces of land. Maveli laughed and said *take it.* The dwarf grew to fill the universe and measured off earth, sky, and then — the third step — placed his foot on Maveli's head and pushed him underground.

Maveli went. He did not fight. He had given his word.

He has been underground ever since. Every year at Onam, Kerala opens its doors for him. Flower carpets are laid. Feasts are cooked. His people remember him in the way people remember something they have always wanted back. He visits. He sees everything. He returns below before the harvest moon sets.

For a thousand years, that was enough. He watched his people. He watched their rivers and their forests and their festivals. He was patient the way only someone with that much time can be patient.

---

**What Maveli knows about Kanjiravanam:**

The five sacred trees that sealed the Kazhukan portal were planted during his reign. He did not plant them — his grove priests did — but he knew every tree, every guardian family, every ritual. The seal was part of the architecture of his kingdom's peace.

When the seal cracked fifty years ago, Maveli felt it. He is underground. He is not merely *in* the underground — Vamana's third step did not simply exile him. It gave him dominion. Every root that runs through Kerala's soil runs through his kingdom. Every crack in the earth is a door he can open or close. He felt the moment Mooppan Velu's grove opened, and he watched what followed — five Kazhukans spreading through five groves over five decades, the soil blackening season by season, the forest that was once the healthiest in his old kingdom slowly going wrong.

He has been watching the whole time. He cannot cross into the mortal world — that is the cost of the third step, and Maveli does not break bargains. But the earth between the mortal world and Pathalam? That is his.

He has been watching LungiMan since the first grove fell.

Every Onam for fifty years, he has arrived in a Muruthikudi that smells of corruption seeping out of Kanjiravanam. He sees the forest. He sees the village that sits beside it, going about its quiet guilty life. He sees the chai stall and the toddy shop and the houseboat on the river.

He has been waiting for fifty years.

---

**The decision:**

After Act IV — four groves freed, Karinkanni's eye burst, the monsoon rain turning clean — Maveli watches LungiMan standing at the edge of the flooded mangrove. He can see the Nilavilakku at LungiMan's belt burning low. He can feel, through the earth, the roots of the fifth sacred tree ahead — and the weight of Pey Komban pacing through that grove. The largest Kazhukan. The one closest to the portal.

He makes a calculation. Not a cold one. A fatherly one.

This man cannot go into that grove like this. Not carrying four fights' worth of damage against something that kills in one charge. And there is something else: Maveli has been watching from below for fifty years, unable to give anyone anything. He has a blessing to give. He has been saving it. He has dominion over this earth. He has one thing he can do.

He opens the ground.

---

**Maveli is not angry.** This is important. He is Kerala's king and he is constitutionally, cosmically incapable of the kind of festering resentment that the Kazhukans live in. He has the patience of someone who was pushed underground with a smile because he had given his word and his word meant something. He is the warmest presence in the coldest place in the game.

But he is also honest. When he meets LungiMan in Pathalam — this battered coconut tapper he brought down himself, who has been fighting for hours with a sickle and a bag of fruit — he does not comfort him with small lies. He tells him what the fifth tree means. He tells him the portal is right there. He tells him to go finish it.

He also knows that when LungiMan seals the fifth tree, he will be able to come home for the first time in fifty years not as a visitor — but to a home that is clean again.

He doesn't say that. But it's in every word.

---

## The Sin That Started Everything

Fifty years ago, a man named **Mooppan Velu** — the guardian of the third sacred tree — had a debt he could not pay. His family was desperate. The old beliefs were fading. He didn't think the stories were really true.

He sold logging rights to the edge of his grove.

He told himself it was just the edge. He told himself the tree itself would be fine. He told himself the gods, if they existed, would understand.

The seal cracked.

The Kazhukans sensed it instantly. They began testing the other four trees. The remaining four guardians — good people, faithful people — died trying to reinforce their seals alone, without the fifth. One by one the groves fell. Five Kazhukans slipped through. Each one claimed a sacred grove and corrupted the surrounding forest, widening the crack in the seal a little more each year.

Mooppan Velu watched it happen. He went into the forest. He found a cave near where his tree used to stand clean. He hasn't left in fifty years.

---

## The People Who Knew

Mooppan Velu didn't do it alone. He had help, and the help had ripples.

**Ustad Basheer** — a wandering bard, young then — arranged the debt relief. He found the logging company. He thought he was helping a desperate family. He has been writing songs about what happened ever since, hiding the truth in folk melodies that nobody in the village quite understands. He travels so he doesn't have to sit still with what he knows.

**Captain Biju** — an ex-fighter, broad-shouldered, already trying to leave his past behind — was the boat captain who transported the timber out. He didn't ask what it was or where it was from. He retired the day the job was done, bought a houseboat, and has been on the river ever since. His houseboat is built partly from those planks. He knows every time he steps on the deck.

**Brother Thoma** — a young seminary student newly arrived from the city — witnessed the deal being signed. He didn't understand the spiritual stakes. He thought it was a property transaction. He said nothing. He has been praying at every forest shrine he can find ever since, as if enough prayer can undo one moment of silence.

**Mundakkal Ravi** — the toddy shop owner, always laughing, never quiet — bought some of the timber to renovate his shop. He didn't know what it was. By the time he suspected, the renovation was done. His shop sits on planks from a sacred grove. Every night when the last customer leaves and the shop goes quiet, he hears something in that silence he cannot name. So he opens another bottle and keeps talking.

**Soniya Chechi** — the chai stall owner — built her stall on the cleared land after the logging. The first years were the best years of her business. She has known something was wrong for decades, ever since the chai stopped tasting quite right. The forest water has been tainted since the seal cracked. She still has one clean spring. She saves it.

None of them talk about it. None of them connect what they did to what the forest became. Or if they do, they don't say so. Muruthikudi is a village that has learned to live alongside its guilt quietly.

---

## LungiMan

His name isn't important to him. The village calls him **LungiMan** — the *thenginkeri*, the coconut tapper. He makes his living climbing coconut palms every day using a rope loop his grandfather taught him, a *cheppam*, slung around his feet and the trunk so he can walk straight up a tree like it's flat ground. He's been doing it since he was twelve. The rope is always at his hip. The small curved *arivaal* — a sickle for cutting coconuts — is always at his waist.

**He looks like this:** Muscular, bare-chested, warm brown skin. White mundu in Kerala working style — half-folded at the waist, gold border doubled at the fold, thighs free for climbing. A brown leather satchel across one shoulder with a green coconut inside. Dark curly hair, thick black mustache. **Aviator sunglasses** — always on, never explained. Barefoot.

He is not a warrior. He is not brave in the heroic sense. He climbs trees for a living and he has a decent arm for throwing coconuts. That is the full extent of his qualifications for what is about to happen to him.

He is also, without knowing it, the only person in Muruthikudi with no connection to what happened fifty years ago. He wasn't born yet. His family had nothing to do with the logging deal. He is clean in a way that nobody else in the village is.

Bhadrakali noticed.

---

## The Blessing — The Swinging Awakening

On an ordinary morning, LungiMan climbed into Kanjiravanam for a routine harvest. A branch broke. He fell deeper into the forest than any tapper had been in living memory, into a grove where the ground was black and the air was wrong and something ancient was watching.

He should have died.

Instead, the forest goddess **Bhadrakali** appeared to him in a vision. Not gentle. Not reassuring. Bhadrakali is the goddess of the forest, of time, of the reckoning that comes when things have been wrong for too long. She appeared because the Kazhukans were close to tearing the seal open completely, and she had run out of better options.

She showed him what had happened. The ancient seal. The five trees. The crack. The fifty years of corruption spreading through Kanjiravanam grove by grove. She showed him five sacred trees, each one held hostage by a Kazhukan who had grown powerful and comfortable in its corrupted grove.

She gave him:
- The agility he already had, but more — beyond what any mortal climber should be capable of
- The ability to feel the forest itself, to read it, to hear it when it warns him
- The mandate to find each of the five sacred trees and drive out the Kazhukan holding it

She did not ask if he wanted this. She did not explain the fifty-year backstory. She showed him enough to understand what needed doing, and then she was gone.

He climbed out of the grove. He went home. He made tea. He didn't sleep that night, or the next.

In the morning, he picked up his rope and his arivaal, and he went back to the forest.

---

## The Village Responds

Here is what nobody tells LungiMan, and what he never quite figures out:

Every person in Muruthikudi who helps him has a reason that isn't kindness.

When **Captain Biju** meets him at the prologue and presses a coconut into his hands and says *"The forest is restless again. I know exactly when it changed"* — that last sentence is a confession that goes nowhere. Captain Biju ferries LungiMan without charging, without explaining, because he is paying a debt he has never spoken aloud.

When **Brother Thoma** gives him a resurrection token at the village shrine and says *"Stay on the trees. The ground belongs to them now"* — the word *now* is doing a lot of work. He knows when the ground stopped being safe. He was there.

When **Mundakkal Ravi** hands him toddy and talks loudly about everything and anything — the noise is the point. Ravi never stops talking because the silence is where the guilt lives.

When **Ustad Basheer** appears at crossroads and riverbanks, singing songs that seem like folk tales but contain actual coordinates to what LungiMan needs — those songs are fifty years of private confession set to music.

When **Soniya Chechi** serves him chai brewed from her last clean spring — she is giving him something she has been saving, rationing, protecting for exactly this moment without knowing it.

They all help him. None of them explain why. LungiMan thinks his village is unusually kind. The player, paying attention, understands something different.

## The Journey — Act by Act

### Prologue: The Swinging Awakening
*Muruthikudi village. Golden hour. The day after.*

LungiMan moves through the village edge for the first time with Bhadrakali's blessing, learning what his body can now do. Everything is still warm and familiar — the chai stall, the village paths, the riverside. Captain Biju gives him a coconut and a warning. Brother Thoma gives him a token and a heavier warning.

He crosses the river. He fights his first Coconut Crab — a thrall, a lesser Kazhukan spirit too small to hold a grove, just large enough to be dangerous. At the edge of the village, the forest deepens. The golden hour ends. He goes in.

---

### Act I: Yakshi's Hollow
*Bamboo groves. Dusk into nightfall.*

The first corrupted grove is a bamboo hollow by the river bend, held by **Yakshi** — a seductress spirit who has flooded the area with illusions. Mirror clones of LungiMan move through the mist. False paths loop back on themselves. The river is treacherous. A broken bridge forces him to use his rope and the trees the way Bhadrakali intended.

Soniya Chechi's chai stall appears in the middle of the corruption — she set up here years ago on the edge of what used to be safe land. She serves him chai from her clean spring and doesn't ask questions.

The boss fight: Yakshi casts a hypnosis curse that reverses his controls, turning his own muscle memory against him. Soniya's chai breaks it. Three clean coconut throws to her hovering form.

When she falls, the bamboo grove breathes. The black soil begins to show green underneath. One grove freed. Four remain.

---

### Act II: Kuttichathan's Carnival
*Abandoned fire festival grounds. Night.*

**Kuttichathan** — a child-spirit, a mischief demi-god — has claimed the abandoned carnival grounds and filled them with fire, illusion, and chaos. He is genuinely gleeful about all of it. He is not malevolent the way Yakshi was. He's having the time of his life, and that somehow makes him worse.

Mundakkal Ravi appears here too, with his motorcycle. He drove here on his Royal Enfield Bullet before the trees got too thick. He lends LungiMan the bike for the approach through the festival grounds — two kilometres of weaving through fire and crowds and Kuttichathan's mischief — before the forest closes in and the bike won't go further.

The carnival grounds are full of Kuttichathan's clones, each one an explosion waiting to happen if you hit the wrong one. The real one flickers. Fire rains from the sky as the boss fight approaches.

Kuttichathan's weakness: he's laughing too hard to be careful. He rides a fireball charge and lands stunned for exactly 0.8 seconds. That's the window.

When he falls, he shrinks into a coconut. The ember-lit ruins go dark and cool. Two groves freed.

---

### Act III: Odiyan's Hunt
*Foggy highlands. Deep night.*

**Odiyan** is the most dangerous of the five because he is patient. A shapeshifter who moves between human form, bull, and dog — and who is immune to attack except during the half-second when he is between forms.

This is the act where **Mooppan Velu** appears. He lives in a cave near the third sacred tree — the one he sold. He knows Odiyan's patterns intimately because he has watched the Kazhukan settle into his grove over fifty years. He speaks in parables. He gives LungiMan four hoof-print markers to find — reading them reveals Odiyan's transform patterns and extends the vulnerability window.

He tells LungiMan everything about Odiyan. He says nothing about why he knows so much. He will not say it. Saying it would make it real in a way that fifty years of cave-dwelling penance has not.

**Kili** — a spirit crow sent by Bhadrakali — appears here too, perching at a shrine. Kili watches. Kili caws one second before each transform. Kili was sent because Bhadrakali knew LungiMan would need a warning system in a fight where the enemy is invisible until the last moment.

The fourth hoof-print triggers the bull charge — Odiyan comes directly at LungiMan in full fury, the camera pulling back to show the scale of it. One jump. One vault over a root. The fight begins.

When Odiyan falls, he collapses into an oil puddle in the fog. The fog lifts. Stars visible for the first time in decades over this highland. Three groves freed.

---

### Act IV: Karinkanni's Curse
*Rain-drenched mangroves. Rising water.*

**Karinkanni** doesn't fight on the ground. It floats high above the flooded mangroves — a massive eye wrapped in thorns and vines, blinking slowly, firing paralysis rays at anything below. You cannot reach it from the ground. The only path to it is the treetops.

The mangroves here have the tallest trees in Kanjiravanam. Reaching the crowns is the challenge. Timing the open eye is the fight — the eye closes between shots and only opens for 1.5 seconds at a time. Paralysis freezes LungiMan completely when a ray connects. Three coconut throws to the pupil.

**Sr. Devi** — a Buddhist nun who lives near a sacred waterfall — guards a quest here that runs deeper than this act: the temple bell taken by a ghostly houseboat. She is the stillest person in the game. When she speaks about Karinkanni, every word is considered.

When the eye bursts, the monsoon rain continues but it's clean rain for the first time. The taint is leaving the water. Four groves freed.

LungiMan stands at the edge of the flooded mangrove. Four fights. Four bosses. He is battered, tired, and there is one grove left — the worst one — somewhere ahead in the dark.

Below him, fifty metres of waterlogged soil and ancient stone, Maveli has been watching.

He has seen four groves freed. He has seen this man climb, swing, throw, fight with a sickle and a bag of coconuts against things that should have killed him. He has seen the lamp on LungiMan's belt — the Nilavilakku that represents his life-force — burning low.

Maveli makes a decision.

The ground opens.

---

### Act IV.5: Pathalam
*Underground. Below everything. Where the old king waits.*

The fall is not a fall. The ground simply opens beneath his feet, and the earth moves *around* him, not past him — like being carried down, not dropped. Like something enormous and gentle rearranging the world to make room.

LungiMan lands in a place that has no right to exist beneath a flood-soaked mangrove. Ancient stone. Kerala temple carvings on every surface — elephants, lotus, faces of gods he half-recognises from shrines he walked past without stopping. An underground river runs cyan-white. Oil lamps float without wind. No enemies. No urgency. Just the weight of the earth above and the quiet of a kingdom that has been patient for a very long time.

He swings between glowing stalactite columns using the same cheppam rope that got him through four acts. The mechanics are the same. The world is inverted.

At the far end, a throne room. Carved from a single piece of ancient black granite. Oil lamps everywhere. And on the throne — a figure. Large. Regal. Watching.

Four boss fights. Nobody comes out of that fresh. The worn-down state is not a player failure — it is simply what four brutal encounters do to a person. Maveli knows this. He pulled LungiMan down because he could see it from below.

---

A warm golden light fills the cave from the direction of the throne. A voice comes from everywhere:

*"I brought you down. I hope that was acceptable."*

LungiMan reaches the throne room. **Maveli** — Mahabali, the legendary king of Kerala's golden age, the man Vamana pushed underground a thousand years ago — sits looking at him. He is enormous and kind and he has the eyes of someone who has not stopped caring despite everything.

He looks at LungiMan for a long moment. Takes in the aviators. The battered satchel. The arivaal. The exhaustion that has nothing to do with HP — the specific tiredness of a man who has been fighting something bigger than himself for hours and has one fight left.

*"My people send me visitors through Onam every year. Flower garlands, feasts, lamps. None of them came down looking like this."*

A pause. The oil lamps breathe.

*"I have been watching since the first grove fell. I watched you take four of them. I know what is ahead."*

He stands. The lamps burn brighter.

*"Kanjiravanam was protected once. By better men than either of us. They are gone now. You are what is left."*

He places his hand on LungiMan's chest. LungiMan's HP fills completely. The Nilavilakku lamp on the HUD blazes — golden now, not orange. Maveli's blessing layers on Bhadrakali's. It will last through everything ahead.

*"Go up. Finish it. Onam is coming — my people need a home to come back to."*

A stone staircase rises from the floor. LungiMan climbs out. Pathalam seals behind him.

---

The rain has stopped when LungiMan surfaces. The sky is still dark but the storm is gone. There is a grove ahead. Something immense is moving in it.

Its footsteps shake the earth.

---

### Act V: Pey Komban's Rampage
*Sacred temple grove. The portal's mouth. Near-black sky.*

**Pey Komban** is the last and the largest. A tusked demon with the mass of a building, dark-skinned, armoured in its own density. Every step it takes shakes the ancient banyan grove. The portal — the original crack in the seal — is visible here, sickly green light bleeding through the roots of the fifth sacred tree.

Ground combat is not a losing proposition. It is not a proposition at all. One charge from Pey Komban at full speed is fatal. The only safe place is the trees.

Brother Thoma is at the temple gate. He grabs LungiMan's arm as he passes and says: *"Stay. On. The. Trees. Promise me."* He means it more than LungiMan knows.

Soniya Chechi has brought everything she has left — all her powerups, the last of the clean chai — in a cart at the grove entrance. *"I brought everything I have. Don't you dare die."*

The fight is three phases. Pey Komban patrols. Pey Komban winds up. Pey Komban charges at 420 pixels per second through anything in its path. The damage window is during the patrol — coconuts from the treetops, LungiMan swinging on his rope in the dark, the ancient banyan branches holding him above the catastrophe below.

When Pey Komban falls, it dissolves into golden light at the banyan's roots. The portal closes. The fifth sacred tree blooms. All five groves begin healing simultaneously.

---

## The Ending

The fireflies rise. All of them, from all five groves, converging on the temple banyan in a white swarm.

Bhadrakali speaks. Not in a vision this time — in the sound of the forest itself. *"Kanjiravanam breathes again."*

The five sacred trees bloom in sequence, each one the colour of the act where it was freed — teal from the bamboo hollow, amber from the carnival grounds, indigo from the highland fog, deep green from the monsoon mangroves, gold from the temple grove. The corruption retreats. The backwater water runs clean. The forest that has been sick for fifty years begins to recover.

Brother Thoma, at the gate: *"I told you. Stay on the trees."*  
Soniya Chechi: *"...want chai?"*

LungiMan goes home. He has no idea what he was part of. He knows he drove five demons out of five groves and sealed a portal, but he doesn't know about the logging deal. He doesn't know why everyone helped him the way they did. He doesn't know that every person in his village carries a piece of what went wrong.

He just knows the forest feels different now. Lighter. Like it's sleeping instead of waiting.

He picks up his rope. There are coconuts to harvest.

In Pathalam, Maveli sits on his throne in the lamplight. The fifth tree is sealed. The portal is closed. The forest his grove priests planted their roots into — the forest he has been watching go dark for fifty years from below — is clean again.

Onam is in three weeks.

From this year, he will come home wholeheartedly.

## What The Player Knows (That LungiMan Doesn't)

For the attentive player, the full story assembles itself in fragments:

- Captain Biju's line: *"I know exactly when it changed"* — not "the forest changed," *I know when*
- Ustad Basheer's songs in Act III contain actual coordinates and names, if you read the lyrics
- Mooppan Velu never explains himself, only explains the demons — as if he owes that information
- Mundakkal Ravi's shop, if you look at it in Act II, has timber grain that doesn't match the surrounding buildings
- Soniya Chechi's chai tastes different in Act V from Act I — she switched to the last clean spring
- Brother Thoma's resurrection token is not a gift. It's atonement in the shape of an object.

Nobody confesses. Nobody is forgiven. The forest heals. The guilt remains.

The village of Muruthikudi goes on.

*"The five sacred trees hold the world in balance. The demons have waited centuries.  
All that stands between them and the mortal realm is one carefree toddy-tapper  
with an arivaal, a bag of coconuts, and very bad judgment."*
